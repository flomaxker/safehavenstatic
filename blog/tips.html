Making Dutch Fun: Easy First Steps to Introduce the Language to Your Expat Child in Groningen

Moving to a new country brings many exciting opportunities—and for expat families in Groningen, learning Dutch is an important step for social and academic integration. Although it might seem challenging at first, introducing your child to Dutch can be enjoyable and straightforward. Here are some practical tips to help you start the journey smoothly:

1. Integrate Dutch into Everyday Life

Label Everyday Items

Attach labels in Dutch to objects around your home, such as stoel (chair), tafel (table), and koelkast (fridge).

Practice Simple Greetings

Use basic greetings like "Hallo!" (hello) and "Doei!" (bye) in daily interactions, helping your child naturally pick up conversational Dutch.

Count Together

Turn daily activities into counting exercises—counting toys, steps, or snacks together in Dutch to reinforce basic vocabulary.

2. Keep it Playful and Interactive

Dutch Songs and Rhymes

Listening to simple children's songs and nursery rhymes in Dutch can significantly enhance vocabulary retention and make language learning fun. You can find popular Dutch children's songs on websites like Kinderliedjes.info.

Games in Dutch

Incorporate Dutch vocabulary into familiar games and playtime activities, such as naming colors, animals, or toys in Dutch.

3. Utilize Visual Learning Tools

Flashcards and Picture Books

Use flashcards with images and corresponding Dutch words. Reading Dutch picture books together can also be an engaging way to introduce new vocabulary and sentence structures. Make sure to visit the wonderful children's bookstore in Groningen, De Kleine Kapitein.

4. Encourage Interaction and Community Activities

Allow your child opportunities to interact with Dutch-speaking peers through playdates, community groups, or activities. Groningen has many international playgroups and activities—check local community boards or social media groups for upcoming events. Make sure to visit the local library, the Groninger Forum. Be aware that you can sign up your child up to age 18 at the Forum—free of charge.

5. Thoughtfully Use Digital Resources

Online Tools

Many apps and online resources offer basic Dutch vocabulary in fun formats. Apps such as Dinolingo and Squla offer child-friendly approaches that make language learning accessible and engaging.

For extra practice with Dutch pronouns, try my Dutch Pronoun Game for a quick challenge.

6. Emphasize Listening Skills

Passive Listening

Playing Dutch audio resources like music, stories, or podcasts in the background helps children absorb the language naturally over time. Audiobooks like those from Nextory for Kids can be particularly useful.

7. Foster Patience and Positivity

Celebrate Progress

Recognize and celebrate each small milestone your child achieves in learning Dutch. A positive, encouraging environment is key to successful language learning.
